01 // BLUF
Bottom Line Up Front
01
[J] Most likely outcome:

CAR is entering a period of managed central continuity but persistent peripheral fragility. Touadéra’s third term is institutionally secured, but that does not equal broad-based political legitimacy. [1]

02
[J] Core analytic judgment:

The principal risk is not immediate regime collapse. It is the widening gap between a consolidated political center and insecure, humanitarian-stressed margins.

03
[F] Security outlook:

Overall security is better than in earlier peak-conflict periods and peace-process gains are real, including the April 2025 N’Djamena agreement with 3R and UPC. Border zones, mining areas, transhumance corridors, and the northeast remain fragile. [6]

04
[F] Humanitarian outlook:

Humanitarian pressure remains severe enough to erode local stability, reduce resilience, and widen recruitment opportunities for armed actors. [2] [10] [12]

05
[J] Decision implication:

Decision-makers should treat CAR as a fragile-consolidation environment requiring simultaneous support to local governance, humanitarian access, and border-area security monitoring.

05 // EVIDENCE
Evidence Summary
What we know
  • Touadéra’s re-election was confirmed at 77.9 percent and he was sworn in on 30 March 2026 for a new seven-year term. [3] [4]
  • MINUSCA reported improved overall security but continued fragility in border areas, mining sites, transhumance corridors, and the northeast. [6]
  • OCHA, WHO, and UNHCR reporting points to 2.3 million people in need, 1.92 million facing high acute food insecurity, and large-scale displacement. [2] [10] [12]
What we assess
  • The political center is stronger than the periphery. Institutional control exceeds inclusive legitimacy.
  • Security progress is real but uneven. Bangui-centered success narratives likely overstate durability in border and communal-friction zones.
  • Humanitarian contraction is more likely to amplify local grievance and recruitment vulnerability than to trigger immediate national rupture.
What we do not know
  • The full independent electoral-forensics picture beyond court rulings and major media reporting. [3]
  • Current district-level trend data across all peripheral prefectures.
  • Whether aid shortfalls will remain manageable through reprioritization or trigger sharper service collapse in specific zones. [8]

Key Drivers

[FACT] Central institutional continuity after the disputed election. [FACT] Uneven security gains with persistent peripheral fragility. [FACT] Severe humanitarian need and food insecurity. [INFERENCE] A widening center-periphery legitimacy gap is likely to be the main risk multiplier over the next six months.

Binding Constraints

Structural: weak local state reach and high border exposure. Political: inclusion deficits and boycott-linked legitimacy questions. Resource: humanitarian underfunding and access strain. Temporal: short lead time for peripheral deterioration to outpace response.

Scenario Modeling

Best Case

Central continuity is matched by modest local governance extension, sustained DDR progress, and stabilized humanitarian access. Result: fragile but improving consolidation. [6]

Base Case

Bangui remains stable while peripheral insecurity, humanitarian distress, and legitimacy deficits persist. Result: no national rupture, but high subnational fragility and recurring shocks.

Worst Case

Localized violence, Sudan spillover, abusive security responses, and aid contraction compound into wider fragmentation and a harder center-periphery disconnect.

07 // BEHAVIORAL
Applied Behavioral Tradecraft

Justification note: Behavioral analysis is relevant because legitimacy signaling, grievance formation, and perceived protection gaps shape whether peripheral stress remains containable or becomes politically consequential.

Driver 1: Legitimacy signaling gap

The presidency can signal institutional victory but not necessarily inclusive legitimacy. Boycott and fraud narratives preserve a grievance mobilization opening. [3]

Driver 2: Center-periphery perception split

Peripheral communities are likely to judge the state less by formal electoral outcomes and more by everyday protection, access, and treatment by armed or state-linked actors. [7]

Driver 3: Coercive stability risk

Where security improvements rely on coercive actors or abusive local proxies, short-term order can degrade long-term trust and cohesion. [7]

Driver 4: Aid-expectation shock

In assistance-dependent zones, funding contraction can turn unmet expectations into grievance and susceptibility to political or armed manipulation. [2]

Influence & Narrative Vulnerabilities

The main narrative collision is between “stability has returned” and “the political system is closed while the periphery remains abandoned.” If the second narrative becomes more credible locally, legitimacy losses will outpace formal control gains. [11]
