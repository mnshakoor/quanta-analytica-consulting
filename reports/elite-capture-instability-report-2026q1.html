01 // BLUF
Bottom Line Up Front
01

[J] Primary finding: Elite capture is best understood as a conditional driver inside a mixed global relationship, not a universal stand-alone cause and not merely a downstream symptom. The strongest pattern appears where capture converges with weak institutions, exclusion, legitimacy erosion, and low corrective capacity. [1]

02

[F] Global pattern: Transparency International’s 2025 CPI reports a global average of 42 and 122 of 182 countries below 50. V-Dem’s 2026 report identifies 44 autocratizing countries containing 41% of the world’s population. OECD’s States of Fragility 2025 identifies 61 contexts with high and extreme fragility. [6] [8] [2]

03

[J] Strongest cases: The clearest high-capture, high-instability cases in the comparative sample are Sudan, South Sudan, Haiti, Venezuela, and Myanmar. [7] [10] [14]

04

[J] Managed-capture cases: Hungary, Azerbaijan, Georgia, El Salvador, India, Tunisia, and South Africa show substantial institutional distortion without equivalent immediate systemic collapse. [11] [12] [16] [17] [18]

05

[J] Reverse or mixed cases: Ukraine and South Korea show that instability can be acute even when elite capture is not the primary proximate driver of the present crisis. [15] [19]

06

[J] Decision implication: Elite capture matters most because it blocks correction. It weakens courts, procurement integrity, media scrutiny, and security accountability, making shocks harder to absorb and peaceful adjustment less likely. [1] [4]

04 // KEY JUDGMENTS
Ranked Key Judgments
Moderate / KJ-1 | KIQ-1 KIQ-2

Elite capture is meaningfully correlated with instability, but the relationship is conditional rather than universal. The strongest fit is where weak institutions and legitimacy erosion coexist with capture. [1] [4] [10]

Moderate / KJ-2 | KIQ-3

The most destabilizing forms of capture are cross-domain: procurement distortion, selective justice, electoral manipulation, security-sector politicization, and media or narrative control. [4] [11] [14]

Moderate / KJ-3 | KIQ-4 KIQ-5

Managed-capture systems can persist without overt collapse when coercive capacity, external shielding, or patronage financing remain intact. [11] [16] [17] [18]

Moderate / KJ-4 | KIQ-4

Instability can also precede and deepen capture, especially in wartime and constitutional emergencies, but that is not the dominant global pattern. [15] [19]

05 // EVIDENCE
Evidence Summary
What we know
  • OECD defines policy capture as repeatedly directing policy away from the public interest toward a specific interest. [1]
  • World Bank methodology includes capture of the state by elites and private interests within Control of Corruption. [3]
  • World Bank’s state-capture guide treats elite capture as a related phenomenon in which resources are usurped by powerful groups. [4]
  • Current country evidence shows both severe captured-fragile cases and managed-capture cases. [10]
What we assess
  • Elite capture is most destabilizing when it degrades distribution, restraint, and legitimacy simultaneously.
  • The strongest mechanisms are procurement distortion, selective justice, election manipulation, security-sector politicization, media capture, and exclusionary patronage. [4]
  • The relationship is strongest in low-resilience states and hybrid regimes with weak oversight and contested legitimacy. [2] [10]
What we do not know
  • The true depth of covert patronage networks, offshore sheltering, and security-intelligence collusion in closed systems.
  • Whether some managed-capture systems are resilient or delayed-rupture systems.
  • The precise temporal ordering in every case: capture-first, instability-first, or mutually reinforcing.

WSI independence note: Core judgments draw on OECD, World Bank, TI, V-Dem, IMF, OHCHR, official government material, and Reuters. Amber Reuters items were not used alone for load-bearing judgments.

07 // BEHAVIORAL
Applied Behavioral Tradecraft

Behavioral analysis is relevant here because elite capture is sustained not only by material control, but also by legitimacy narratives, fear management, and elite threat perception. [8] [14]

1. Elite threat perception

Ruling coalitions that equate loss of office with prosecution, asset loss, or exclusion are more likely to weaponize courts, media, and security organs.

2. Legitimacy substitution

Where procedural legitimacy weakens, regimes compensate with anti-chaos, cleansing, or security narratives. [16] [18]

3. Fear conditioning

Repeated arrests, sealed charges, or selective prosecutions can suppress dissent without resolving grievance. [14]

4. Narrative normalization of extraction

Clientelist or nationalist frames can rebrand favoritism as sovereignty or stability. V-Dem finds freedom of expression is the most frequently attacked aspect of democracy across autocratizing countries. [8]

Behavioral judgment: The behavioral layer does not replace structural analysis. It explains why some captured systems maintain surface calm for a time: fear, narrative management, and elite cohesion can defer overt rupture while increasing medium-term fragility. [14] [16] [18]

08 // IMPLICATIONS
Strategic Implications, Risk & Recommendations

Governments and multilaterals: technical anti-corruption compliance is insufficient once capture reaches procurement, courts, elections, and security institutions. Reform must target oversight nodes and coercive restraint. [1]

Donors: waiting for overt conflict indicators is too late. Many critical risks appear first as institutional distortion.

Investors and corporate risk teams: managed-capture environments can be more dangerous than visibly chaotic ones because contract enforcement, licensing, and court neutrality deteriorate quietly.

Security planners: captured systems are poor at truthful reporting, corrective feedback, and proportionate de-escalation. That raises miscalculation risk under stress. [4]

Recommendations

0–72H Immediate
  • Audit emergency procurement authorities and case-allocation integrity in courts.
  • Map the oversight nodes most vulnerable to capture: procurement, SOEs, tax, electoral bodies, and justice.
  • Flag politically exposed counterparties, sudden licensing shifts, opaque tenders, and court unpredictability.
3–30 Days Near-Term
  • Harden beneficial-ownership disclosure, prosecutor independence, and election-verification processes.
  • Tie support to verifiable institutional controls rather than generic reform language.
  • Build exposure maps around customs, regulators, SOEs, and security-linked suppliers.
30–180 Days Medium-Term
  • Redesign procurement, public-finance, and judicial governance around transparency and sanction certainty.
  • Protect investigative ecosystems, municipal service delivery, and watchdog capacity.
  • Use trigger-based scenario monitoring rather than annual country-score complacency.
09 // CONFIDENCE
Confidence & Uncertainties
Overall Confidence: Moderate
  • Multiple independent high-quality sources support the core conditional-relationship judgment. [1] [4] [10]
  • The comparative sample includes confirming, disconfirming, and outlier cases, reducing single-story bias.
  • The main uncertainty is measurement, not whether the relationship exists at all. Elite capture remains partly hidden and proxy-dependent. [4]
Better global capture measurement could show weaker correlation than current proxies imply.
Several managed-capture systems may prove more resilient than this assessment currently judges.
External shocks, especially war and macro stress, may explain more instability variance than capture in some regions.
