01 // BLUF
Bottom Line Up Front
01
Managed fragmentation is the most defensible baseline.

The AES-ECOWAS split has increased corridor friction and governance uncertainty, but current evidence supports a hybrid trade order rather than total formal collapse. ECOWAS preserved limited continuity in movement and trade treatment even after the withdrawal became effective.[1][2]

02
Near-term sovereignty gains remain under-supported.

World Bank assessments for Burkina Faso, Mali, and Niger point toward higher financing pressure, trade disruption risk, and institutional friction, while AES policy steps such as the 0.5 percent import levy appear more consistent with cost layering than rapid self-sufficiency.[3][4][5][6]

03
Food and market stress are real, but geographically uneven.

Conflict-exposed markets remain the principal stress points. FEWS NET reporting supports high cereal prices and supply disruption in insecure areas, while some conflict-unaffected areas of Niger have shown more easing than the wider regional narrative implies.[8][9][10]

04
The highest strategic risk is criminalized regionalization.

As corridor visibility weakens, trafficking networks and armed actors gain more room to capture rents, tax movement, or exploit rerouted trade. UNODC-linked seizure data and Reuters reporting on JNIM revenue behavior both reinforce this risk pathway.[11][12]

05
Decision advantage now depends on monitoring which adaptation pathway wins.

The decisive question is whether technical accommodation and alternative routing can contain corridor friction before food stress, trafficking, and coercive rent extraction become the dominant operating logic in the central Sahel.[1][6][7][11]

03 // SNAPSHOT
Situation Snapshot
Who
Burkina Faso, Mali, and Niger within the AES; ECOWAS; corridor traders and transporters; local consumers; trafficking networks; and armed actors operating along insecure routes.[1][2][11][12]
What
A regional political split is increasing uncertainty over customs treatment, market access, and transport predictability while leaving enough legal continuity to prevent a clean economic break.[1][2]
Where
The central Sahel and its interfaces with WAEMU systems, coastal West African routes, and emerging Atlantic access concepts routed through Morocco.[3][4][5][7]
When
Assessment period runs from the effective withdrawal date of 29 January 2025 through the operating environment as of 2 April 2026, with a forward horizon of up to 180 days.[1][2]
Why
The issue combines sovereignty signaling, corridor dependence, insecurity, and institutional fragmentation in economies that are landlocked, import-sensitive, and highly exposed to transport disruption.[3][4][5]
How
The most likely mechanism is layered friction: incomplete regional arrangements, new levies, corridor insecurity, trader rerouting, and rising opportunity for predatory or illicit actors to capture rents.[5][6][11][12]

05 // EVIDENCE
Evidence Summary
What We Know
  • ECOWAS recognized the AES withdrawal on 29 January 2025 and simultaneously preserved limited continuity on movement and trade treatment.[1][2]
  • The AES imposed a 0.5 percent levy on imports from outside the bloc in March 2025.[6]
  • The three Sahel states publicly backed Morocco’s Atlantic access initiative in April 2025.[7]
  • Institutional reporting flags food-price stress and market supply disruption in conflict-affected zones.[8][9][10]
  • UNODC-linked reporting showed a sharp rise in cocaine seizures across the Sahel, while Reuters documented JNIM revenue extraction behavior.[11][12]
What We Assess
  • The trade system is fragmenting faster than it is stabilizing.
  • Informal adaptation by traders is likely expanding even where legal channels remain nominally open.
  • Security-linked trade opacity is becoming more strategically important than the legal exit event itself.
  • Technical accommodation is still possible, but it is not yet the dominant trajectory.
What We Do Not Know
  • The actual proportion of trade volume shifting from formal to informal channels.
  • The exact share of informal-route rents captured by insurgents versus officials, intermediaries, or criminal networks.
  • Whether alternative corridors can materially reduce transaction costs inside the next 12 months.
  • How durable current ECOWAS continuity measures will remain under new political stress.

Key Drivers

FACT Corridor dependence and landlocked exposure remain structural realities for all three AES states.[3][4][5]

FACT Policy friction has increased through new bloc-level trade measures and continued political estrangement from ECOWAS.[1][6][7]

INFERENCE Trader rerouting and informal adaptation are likely expanding because the current governance environment rewards workaround behavior more than rule-based predictability.

INFERENCE Corridor opacity raises the chance that trafficking, predation, and coercive rent extraction will become more mutually reinforcing over time.

Binding Constraints on Available Responses

Structural Landlocked geography, corridor dependence, and weak state reach in insecure zones limit rapid policy gains.

Political Sovereignty signaling raises the cost of visible policy reversal even when technical accommodation would ease friction.

Resource Limited logistics, financing, and domestic processing capacity constrain fast substitution of lost trade efficiencies.

Temporal Market stress can intensify faster than institutional workarounds can be negotiated or implemented.

07 // BEHAVIORAL
Applied Behavioral Tradecraft

Behavioral analysis is relevant here because sovereignty signaling, trader adaptation, and legitimacy competition all shape how actors respond to corridor friction and uncertainty.

1

Sovereignty framing and elite commitment

AES leadership has strong incentive to frame separation as autonomy and dignity. That raises the political cost of visible reversal even when practical market friction persists.[1][2][6]

Analytic inference: policy rigidity may outlast immediate market utility because identity signaling is itself a political output.
2

Trader normalization of workaround behavior

When rules become uncertain and routes insecure, traders rationally normalize side payments, rerouting, and off-book transactions. This does not require ideology. It only requires repeated exposure to unreliable formal systems.

Analytic inference: once workaround behavior becomes routine, restoring legal-channel trust becomes harder and slower.
3

Legitimacy competition around corridor control

Where states cannot guarantee predictable passage, non-state actors can gain influence by offering coercive but reliable access, protection, or dispute resolution along routes.[11][12]

Analytic inference: even partial non-state reliability can erode state legitimacy faster than formal policy messaging can repair it.

Influence and Narrative Vulnerabilities

External corridor initiatives can be framed domestically as proof that separation is working even before measurable trade gains appear. Conversely, any short-term easing in selected markets can be over-generalized into a broader sovereignty-success narrative, masking continued distortion in insecure or marginal zones.[7][8][9][10]

08 // IMPLICATIONS
Strategic Implications, Risk, and Recommendations

Strategic Implications

Local

Consumers, traders, and market-dependent communities are likely to absorb the first-order costs through higher transaction uncertainty, localized price spikes, and weaker visibility into who controls route access.

Regional

For ECOWAS and neighboring states, the main strategic challenge is no longer the formal exit itself. It is whether corridor governance can still compete with off-book adaptation and coercive extraction.

International

External partners should expect a more fragmented operating environment in which trade, access, food security, and security assistance become more tightly coupled than traditional sector stovepipes allow.

Recommendations

0-72H Immediate

Use the core line: the AES-ECOWAS split is producing managed fragmentation and informal diversion, not total formal trade collapse.

Treat exact volume claims on smuggling or corridor diversion as unknown unless independently verified.

3-30 Days Near-Term

Track trade, food, and security indicators together rather than in separate analytic lanes.

Build a corridor watchlist focused on markets and routes where price stress and predation overlap.

30-180 Days Medium-Term

Maintain standing scenarios for managed accommodation, managed fragmentation, and criminalized regionalization.

Prioritize collection on who captures rents from informal adaptation and whether alternative routing through Morocco becomes operationally meaningful.

09 // CONFIDENCE
Confidence and Uncertainties
Moderate Overall confidence

Reason 1: The political baseline is clear. The withdrawal became effective on 29 January 2025 and ECOWAS also preserved limited continuity.[1][2]

Reason 2: The macroeconomic direction is consistently supported by institutional reporting across Burkina Faso, Mali, and Niger.[3][4][5]

Reason 3: The food-stress and trafficking dimensions are independently supported even if the exact scale of informal diversion remains unquantified.[8][9][10][11][12]

Uncertainties That Could Flip the Assessment

Uncertainty 1

A substantive ECOWAS-AES technical accommodation could lower corridor friction faster than expected.

Uncertainty 2

Alternative routing through Morocco or other access arrangements could begin to reduce costs in a measurable way within the forecast horizon.

Uncertainty 3

Stronger-than-expected evidence may show that informal diversion is economically manageable and not heavily captured by coercive actors.
