01 // Purpose
Why this course comes next

Course Positioning

This course teaches the analyst what to do after Key Intelligence Questions are established. It turns framed uncertainty into a disciplined evidence workflow that can support analytic judgment, structured tradecraft, and final deliverables.

The training is designed for junior analysts, research assistants, OSINT beginners, NGO risk analysts, corporate intelligence teams, and cross-sector learners who need a repeatable method for building a defensible evidence base.

Primary Promise

By the end of the course, the student can move from KIQs to collection requirements, validate source credibility, extract atomic claims, organize evidence, and prepare the material for ACH, indicators and warnings, scenarios, and written intelligence products.

02 // Learning Objectives
What the learner should be able to do
  • Translate Key Intelligence Questions into collection requirements and information needs.
  • Distinguish primary, secondary, and tertiary sources and assign them appropriate evidentiary weight.
  • Validate sources using access, reliability, provenance, manipulation risk, timeliness, independence, and specificity.
  • Test whether multiple reports represent genuinely independent corroboration or only one upstream stream.
  • Extract atomic claims from long-form articles, official releases, datasets, and technical reporting.
  • Separate fact, inference, assumption, and unknowns in a disciplined evidence log.
  • Build a source register, claim log, assumptions register, and indicators tracker.
  • Use LLM platforms to support extraction, contradiction spotting, and organization without outsourcing judgment.
  • Identify common sourcing failures such as echo chambers, aggregation loops, partisan distortion, and synthetic confidence.
  • Prepare an evidence base that can support ACH, indicators and warnings, scenario work, and final product drafting.
03 // Module Architecture
Training sequence
  • Module 1: Why Evidence Discipline Matters Introduces why source quality matters more than volume and how weak evidence produces narrative drift.
  • Module 2: From KIQs to Collection Requirements Shows how to convert framed questions into concrete information needs and prioritized collection tasks.
  • Module 3: Understanding Source Types Explains primary, secondary, and tertiary sources and how evidentiary weight should change by source type.
  • Module 4: Source Validation and Credibility Testing Teaches structured source evaluation, downgrade triggers, and deception-aware screening.
  • Module 5: Triangulation and Independence Testing Prevents false corroboration by teaching independence checks, shared upstream detection, and stream counting.
  • Module 6: Extracting Atomic Claims Trains the analyst to turn long-form material into checkable claims for comparison and verification.
  • Module 7: Building the Analyst Workflow Provides the source register, claim log, assumptions register, and indicators tracker architecture.
  • Module 8: Using LLMs to Assist the Evidence Workflow Shows how to use LLMs as constrained assistants for extraction, comparison, and organization.
  • Module 9: Preparing Evidence for Analysis Bridges evidence handling into ACH, indicators and warnings, scenario work, and final writing.
  • Module 10: Capstone Workflow Integrates the entire method into a start-to-finish exercise that simulates real analyst production.
